# People

Thinkers and practitioners worth following in information theory, communication, classification, cybernetics, and related fields.

---

## Claude E. Shannon
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon

Mathematician and electrical engineer. Founded information theory with "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" (1948), which established the mathematical framework for quantifying information, channel capacity, and noise. Often called the father of information theory.

Books: [A Mathematical Theory of Communication](books.md#a-mathematical-theory-of-communication)

---

## Niklas Luhmann
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niklas_Luhmann

German sociologist and systems theorist. Developed a theory of social systems in which communication — not people — is the basic unit of social reality. Also notable for his Zettelkasten (slip-box) method, which has become influential in knowledge management.

Books: [What is Communication](books.md#what-is-communication)

---

## Mark S. Granovetter
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Granovetter

American sociologist. "The Strength of Weak Ties" (1973) demonstrated that acquaintances (weak ties) are often more important than close friends (strong ties) for transmitting novel information across social networks — because weak ties bridge otherwise disconnected clusters.

Books: [The Strength of Weak Ties](books.md#the-strength-of-weak-ties)

---

## Birger Hjørland
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birger_Hj%C3%B8rland

Danish library and information scientist. Works on the theoretical foundations of indexing, classification, and knowledge organisation. Advocates for a domain-analytic approach — the idea that the meaning and organisation of information is always relative to a community of practice, not universal.

Books: [Indexing: Concepts and Theory](books.md#indexing-concepts-and-theory)

---

## John Boyd
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Boyd_(military_strategist)

US Air Force fighter pilot and military strategist. Best known for the OODA loop (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act), but "Destruction and Creation" (1976) is the more foundational work — a short paper on how the mind builds and breaks mental models by decomposing observations across domains and synthesising new patterns. Influenced military strategy, business strategy, and systems thinking.

Books: [Destruction and Creation](books.md#destruction-and-creation)
